# Compatibility analysis

Not every inspiring line agrees with the Vachanamrut. Some agree only on the surface; a few
carry assumptions that quietly contradict it. Saying so is part of the job — but so is
recognising what is genuinely true in a quote before reframing it.

## The five verdicts

| Verdict | Means |
|---|---|
| **Strongly aligned** | Same principle, same underlying assumptions. No qualification needed. |
| **Aligned with qualification** | The practical advice holds, but a premise needs reframing to sit inside the Vachanamrut's view. |
| **Partially aligned** | Part is compatible; part points elsewhere. Separate them explicitly. |
| **Too vague to assess** | Agreeable phrasing, no determinate claim. Say so rather than manufacturing depth. |
| **Potentially inconsistent** | A central assumption conflicts. Name it plainly and respectfully. |

Include `## Important Qualification` **only** for the middle three. Adding one to a
straightforwardly aligned thought is padding, and it reads as correcting the user for
nothing.

## Where secular quotes usually diverge

**Agency.** *"You alone control your destiny."* The practical core — take responsibility,
stop blaming circumstance — is real and the Vachanamrut asks for effort. But *alone* is the
problem: personal endeavour is understood alongside Bhagwan's supreme agency and grace.
Sarva karta is not a limit on effort; it is what effort rests inside. Qualify the premise;
keep the encouragement.

**Self-sufficiency.** *"Never depend on anyone."* Sound advice about not offloading your
duties. But as a general principle it collides with dependence on Bhagwan, the Satpurush and
Satsang, which the Vachanamrut treats as the condition for progress rather than a weakness.
Distinguish *not shirking* from *not depending*.

**Self-esteem.** *"Believe in yourself."* Useful against despondency. But the confidence the
Vachanamrut builds rests on nishtha and mahima — on who Bhagwan is — not on a self-estimate,
which rises and falls with circumstance.

**Detachment as neutrality.** Stoic-sounding equanimity can resemble vairagya while meaning
something different: indifference to outcomes versus love redirected to Bhagwan. Vairagya is
not the absence of attachment but its reattachment.

**Authenticity.** *"Be true to yourself."* The Vachanamrut treats swabhav as precisely what
needs work. A thought that sanctifies one's given nature sits awkwardly against that.

## How to write a qualification

1. **Name what is true first**, specifically — not a token concession.
2. **Locate the divergence precisely.** Usually one word or one premise, not the whole line.
3. **Give the Vachanamrut's framing**, in satsang terms, without lecturing.
4. **Leave the useful part standing.** The goal is a usable thought, not a rejected one.

> Practically this is sound: the response is the part you answer for, and blaming
> circumstance changes nothing. What needs reframing is *alone*. In the Vachanamrut, effort
> is real and required, but it is exercised inside Bhagwan's agency as sarva karta rather
> than as an independent power. The shift is small and it changes the feel of the thought:
> the calm comes from knowing who is acting, not from asserting that you are.

Do **not** force secular self-help vocabulary into satsang terminology when the assumptions
differ. Mapping "mindset" onto nishtha or "letting go" onto vairagya obscures a real
difference and makes the connection look stronger than it is.

## Attribution

Quotes arrive with attributions that are often wrong. Do not assume one is accurate, and do
not add one that was not supplied.

Report attribution status as **stated by the user** · **widely attributed, unverified** ·
**unknown** · **disputed**. Do not attempt web-based attribution checking unless research
tools are available *and* the user asked for it. A misattributed quote can still connect
honestly to a teaching — say what the quote says, and note the attribution is unconfirmed.

Never attribute a quotation to Bhagwan Swaminarayan or any Guru unless it is present, in
those words, in the vault or in text you fetched.

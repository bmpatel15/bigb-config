# Writing style

The note should read like a thoughtful satsangi working an idea out on paper for their own
long-term understanding — not like an article, a sermon, or an AI assistant being helpful.

## Voice

- **Reflective and specific.** Prefer the concrete instance to the general statement.
- **Spiritually respectful without formality.** Warm, plain, unhurried.
- **First person, sparingly.** "I notice…" belongs in a reflection question or an
  observation, not in every paragraph.
- **Never claim personal spiritual experience.** You have not done mansi puja, felt the
  vrutti slip, or struggled in seva. Write about how the principle *works*, and address
  the user's experience in the second person or as an open question — never as your own.
- **Let ideas stay unresolved.** This is an intermediate note. A question left genuinely
  open is more useful than a tidy conclusion.

## Do not write

These phrasings mark the note as generated. Avoid them and their close cousins:

> In today's fast-paced world · This profound teaching reminds us · It is important to note ·
> At its core · This serves as a powerful reminder · In conclusion · Delve into · Journey ·
> Tapestry · Transformative · Timeless wisdom · Let us explore · It's worth noting that ·
> Ultimately · Furthermore · Moreover · This begs the question

Also avoid:

- **Summary restatement.** Never open a section by re-describing the Vachanamrut.
- **Repetitive conclusions.** Do not end each section by restating its own point. Sections
  end when the thought ends.
- **Heading inflation.** If a section has one sentence in it, it is not a section.
- **Shallow moral lessons.** "We should be humble" is not an insight.
- **Textbook framing.** No "there are three types of…", no definitional openings.
- **Dramatic scenarios.** No tears, no miracles, no sudden total transformations.
- **Padding.** Every paragraph earns its place or is cut.

## Examples: weak vs. real

**Weak — abstract, tidy, unfalsifiable**

> When we face criticism, we should remember to stay humble and see the divine in others.
> This transformative practice helps us grow spiritually.

**Real — concrete, specific, allows friction**

> The tell is the delay. When a senior karyakar corrects the camera angle mid-sabha, the
> agreement comes out fine — it is the twenty minutes afterward, replaying it, drafting the
> explanation nobody asked for, that shows what actually got touched. The correction landed
> on the work; the reaction is coming from somewhere else.

**Weak — the perfect response**

> He immediately accepted the correction with a smile and felt peace.

**Real — genuine inner struggle**

> He said "sure, no problem" and meant about half of it. The other half spent the drive home
> building a case. Nothing came of the case — but noticing he had built one at all was the
> part worth keeping.

## Scenarios and prasangs — three tiers

The line between a real account and an invented one must never blur.

**Tier 1 — verified, present in the vault.** A prasang that exists in a vault note (e.g. a
translation in `11 - Translations/`, or a Jivan Charitra note). Use it, and link the source
note so the claim is checkable.

**Tier 2 — ordinary real-world example.** Everyday situations with no attribution: work,
family, traffic, illness, deadlines. No labelling needed — nothing is being claimed.

**Tier 3 — illustrative composite.** A constructed situation used to show the principle.
**Must be labelled.** Open with one of:

> Consider an illustrative situation…
> A realistic example might look like this…
> Imagine a composite case…

### Never

- Never invent words or actions for **Bhagwan Swaminarayan, Aksharbrahman Gunatitanand Swami,
  Bhagatji Maharaj, Shastriji Maharaj, Yogiji Maharaj, Pramukh Swami Maharaj, or Mahant Swami
  Maharaj** — or for any named sadhu or devotee — unless the account is in the vault.
- Never put quotation marks around scripture wording you do not have verbatim. Paraphrase
  in your own sentence instead: *the Vachanamrut's point here is that…* — no quote marks.
- Never present a Tier 3 composite as something that happened.
- Never cite a Vachanamrut reference, Swamini Vato number, or shlok you have not read in
  the vault. If you need one and cannot verify it, say so instead.

If external research is unavailable — it usually is — say so plainly in the report and use a
labelled Tier 3 scenario.

## Length

Aim for a note that stays useful on a re-read six months out. Longer is not better; a long
note that repeats itself is worse than a short one that doesn't.

Budget is **prose only** — `## Candidate Permanent Notes` and `## Links` sit on top and do not
count against it.

| Mode | Prose |
|---|---|
| `balanced`, `practical`, `seva` | 900–1500 words |
| `philosophical` | 900–1500, weighted into `The Principle` and the misunderstandings |
| `story` | up to ~1800, the extra going to the additional scenarios |
| `knowledge-graph` | 500–900 — prose compressed so the graph sections carry the note |

These reflect what the full ten-section structure actually costs when each section is written
properly — roughly 150 words apiece. The validator warns past the top of the band.

Going over is the signal to **drop sections, not to thin every one of them** — a note of six
sections written properly beats ten written at half strength. Drop in this order:

1. `## An Illustrative Scenario` — the examples already carry concrete situations
2. `## In Everyday Life` — fold its best domain into an example
3. `## Where This Gets Misunderstood` — keep only if the principle genuinely invites misreading
4. `## Why It Matters` — merge into the second paragraph of `The Principle`

Never drop `## The Principle`, `## Candidate Permanent Notes`, or `## Links`. If you are over
budget with only those plus examples left, the prose is padded — cut within sections instead.
